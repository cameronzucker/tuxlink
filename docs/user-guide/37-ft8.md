# FT-8

FT-8 in Tuxlink is a **listener**. It decodes the FT-8 stations your radio can hear and
reports who they are, where they are, and how strong they are. It does not transmit, and
it does not carry messages.

That sounds like a limitation. It is the point.

## What FT-8 is for here

FT-8 is the most sensitive digital mode in common amateur use: it decodes signals well
below the noise floor, far below what an ear or a Winlink modem can pull out. So a band
full of FT-8 traffic is a **live propagation map**. Before committing to a Winlink
connection on 40m, listening to FT-8 on 40m answers the question that actually matters:

> Is this band open right now, and to where?

If FT-8 hears twenty stations across three states on 40m, the band is open. If it hears
nothing on 40m and a dozen stations on 20m, the answer is 20m. That is a measurement,
made with the radio you already have, and it costs no airtime because you never key up.

In an emergency this is the difference between picking a band and guessing one.

## FT-8 carries no message traffic

This surprises people, so it is worth being blunt. An FT-8 transmission is a **77-bit
payload** — not 77 characters, 77 *bits*. It packs a small set of fixed message types:

- a callsign, a callsign, and a Maidenhead grid (`CQ K7ABC DM43`)
- a callsign, a callsign, and a signal report (`K7ABC N7CPZ -12`)
- an acknowledgement (`RRR`, `73`)

There is a free-text type, and it holds **13 characters** from a restricted alphabet.
That is the whole channel.

So FT-8 cannot send an email, cannot send a form, and cannot pass traffic. If you need to
move a message, that is Winlink over ARDOP, VARA, or packet. FT-8 tells you **which band
to do it on**.

## Listening

Open the FT-8 panel and press start. Three things have to be right:

**An audio device.** FT-8 decodes the receive audio coming from your radio, so Tuxlink
needs the sound-card input your interface presents (Digirig, SignaLink, a rig's built-in
USB codec). If the listener reports it is blocked and needs a device, pick one from the
list — see [Digirig](10-digirig.md) and [SignaLink and others](11-signalink-and-others.md).

**The right band.** Setting the band tells Tuxlink which FT-8 segment to expect. If you
have CAT control configured ([CAT and rigctld](12-cat-and-rigctld.md)), setting the band
also moves the radio's dial to that band's FT-8 frequency for you. Without CAT, set the
dial on the radio yourself to match.

**Accurate time.** FT-8 works in synchronised 15-second slots, so your computer's clock
has to be close to correct — within about a second. A clock that has drifted badly will
decode nothing at all, and the symptom looks exactly like a dead band. If FT-8 hears
nothing on a band you believe is open, suspect the clock before the antenna.

## Sweeping

Rather than sitting on one band, the listener can **sweep**: step through several bands
in turn, dwelling on each, building a picture of which ones are actually open. This needs
CAT control, because Tuxlink has to move the dial for you.

Sweeping is the fastest way to answer "which band should I use right now" without
touching the radio.

## Reading the results

Each station you hear comes with:

- **Callsign** — who it is.
- **Grid** — a Maidenhead locator, so you know *where* it is. Grid plus your own grid is
  a bearing and a distance, which is what tells you the band is open *in the direction
  you care about*.
- **SNR** — signal-to-noise in dB. FT-8 reports are usually negative; this is normal.
  −5 dB is a strong FT-8 signal. −20 dB is still perfectly decodable. This is not a
  problem with your station.
- **Times heard** — a station heard repeatedly is a stable path, not a lucky one-off.

Not every station gets decoded cleanly. Some decodes never resolve a callsign; those are
dropped rather than shown, because a station you cannot name is not a station you can use.

## Asking the assistant

Elmer can drive the listener and read it: it can start and stop it, set the band, and
tell you who you are hearing. Questions like *"who am I hearing on 20m?"* or *"is 40m
open?"* are answered from real decodes, not from a guess.

The listener is receive-only, so nothing here transmits, and Elmer does not need transmit
authority to use it.
